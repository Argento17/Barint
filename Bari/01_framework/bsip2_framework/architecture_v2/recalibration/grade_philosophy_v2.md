# Grade Philosophy v2 — S/A/B/C/D/E System

**Status:** Recalibration proposal  
**Version:** 2.0-draft  
**Date:** 2026-05-18  
**Companion:** distribution_analysis.md, recalibration_proposals.md

---

## Why This Document Exists

Bari's current grade thresholds (A=85+, B=70+, C=55+, D=40+, E<40) produce a distribution in which:

- **A grade: 0% of all scored products** — mathematically unreachable in practice
- **B grade: ~8% of all products** — exclusively floor-rescued NOVA 1 single-ingredient foods
- **D+E: ~80% of all products** — across both milk and snack bar corpora

This distribution makes Bari feel like a punishment engine. It does not accurately express Bari's philosophy. A system where 80% of the food landscape lands at D or E is not communicating structural food intelligence — it is communicating ideological pessimism dressed as analysis.

The goal of v2 grade philosophy is not to raise scores. It is to make the grade system **expressive, proportional, and coherent with Bari's stated identity**.

---

## The New Grade System

### S — Structural Elite (90–100)

**What it means:**  
Rare. Reserved for foods whose entire profile is coherent at the highest structural level: single-ingredient or near-single-ingredient whole food, minimal to no processing, intact matrix, meaningful nutritional contribution appropriate to its type, no engineering signals of any kind.

**Examples that should plausibly reach S:**
- Raw almonds, tahini, extra-virgin olive oil (though caloric density tables must accommodate)
- Single-varietal honey in its natural state
- Whole, unprocessed fish or meat (if/when scored)

**Why S exists:**  
The current A ceiling at 85+ is achievable only by products that are simultaneously: NOVA 1, single-ingredient, no missing data, no additives, no red labels, no trans fat signal, and with strong nutrient density. In practice, nearly nothing reaches it because NOVA 1 single-ingredient whole foods frequently have low fiber or low protein (by the current formula's calibration), which drags the weighted dimension score below 85 even before any caps.

S-tier is introduced so that A-tier — 80 to 89 — becomes **realistically achievable** for excellent products without inflating the ceiling. S-tier is the ceiling. A-tier is the reachable benchmark.

**S is not:**
- A tier for every "healthy food"
- Automatically assigned to any NOVA 1 product
- A grade achievable by nutritional fortification (which is a structural substitute, not structural excellence)

---

### A — Structurally Strong (80–89)

**What it means:**  
A product in the A range demonstrates all of the following:
- Minimal to no processing artifacts (NOVA 1–2)
- Coherent food matrix (ingredient list consistent with declared food type)
- Meaningful nutritional contribution appropriate to category
- No or minimal engineering signals
- No active regulatory concern

A is not "perfect food." It is structurally excellent food for what it is. Plain whole milk (NOVA 1, intact matrix, genuine protein, no additives) belongs in A. Plain Greek yogurt (NOVA 1–2, coherent fermented dairy, strong protein) belongs in A. Single-ingredient nut products belong in A if the caloric density table credits them appropriately.

**A is not:**
- Reserved for low-calorie foods (olive oil can be A)
- Reserved for high-fiber foods (dairy doesn't need fiber to be A)
- A guarantee of health benefit in any individual context
- Achievable by fortification alone

**"Structurally excellent" ≠ "perfect for every person or context"**  

A whole-fat dairy product is structurally A: intact matrix, coherent nutrition, no processing concern, no engineering. Whether it is appropriate for a person with a specific intolerance, metabolic condition, or dietary preference is outside Bari's scope. Bari says: "This is what it is, and it is structurally excellent." It does not say: "Eat this."

---

### B — Generally Sound (65–79)

**What it means:**  
B products are structurally coherent with meaningful tradeoffs. They may have:
- Modest processing (NOVA 2–3 range)
- Incomplete nutritional contribution (e.g., plant milks with good matrix but limited protein)
- Minor regulatory signals that are not dominant
- Limited engineering without palatability engineering intent

Simple, unsweetened soy drink (NOVA 2) belongs in B. A fermented dairy product with minor additives belongs in B. A minimally processed snack bar with good structure and no heavy sugar load belongs in B.

**B is not:**
- The grade Bari defaults to when unsure
- The grade that whole-food elites fall back to under unfavorable conditions
- A comfortable middle — it is a genuine endorsement of structural coherence

---

### C — Mixed / Moderate (50–64)

**What it means:**  
C products carry real tradeoffs that matter but are not structurally disqualifying. They may have:
- Processing signals that reduce structural confidence (NOVA 3)
- Limited but not negligible nutritional contribution
- Some additive presence without clear palatability engineering
- Minor to moderate regulatory signals

Oat milk, rice milk, and most plant-based beverage alternatives belong in C: they are coherent processed products that deliver limited nutrition at acceptable structural integrity. A NOVA 3 snack bar with modest sugar and some whole-grain presence belongs in C.

---

### D — Structurally Weak or Compromised (35–49)

**What it means:**  
D products have at least one layer of significant structural failure. This may be:
- Heavy reconstruction (NOVA 4 with extensive additive architecture)
- Severely limited nutritional contribution in a low-calorie matrix
- Multiple engineering signals without compensating structural strength
- Active regulatory concern (e.g., one red label) alongside structural weakness

Plain almond drink (very low protein, NOVA 3–4 reconstruction, limited matrix coherence) belongs in D. An engineered protein shake with artificial sweeteners and flavor agents belongs in D despite genuine protein content.

**D does not mean "avoid this food."**  
D means: Bari's structural analysis finds significant concern in this product's architecture. A user may choose to consume it for reasons entirely outside Bari's scope. Bari names the concern and gives the grade — it does not prescribe behavior.

---

### E — Dominant Concern (below 35)

**What it means:**  
E products have dominant, stacking failures across multiple architectural dimensions:
- Ultra-processed formulation with extensive additive architecture (NOVA 4 + 5+ additive categories)
- High sugar and red label triggers alongside processing failure
- Heavily engineered palatability profile (sweetener + flavor + color + stabilizer combinations)
- Negligible structural contribution relative to calories

A chocolate-coated cereal bar with 2+ Israeli red labels, NOVA 4, 5+ additive categories, and refined added sugar belongs firmly in E. Bari is not being punitive — it is recognizing that this product's architecture, across all four future layers, produces a dominant concern verdict.

**E does not mean "dangerous" or "toxic."**  
Bari does not use alarmist language. E means: Bari's structural analysis finds no dimension where this product demonstrates structural adequacy, and multiple dimensions show active concern. The grade is a structural verdict, not a safety warning.

## The "Punishment Engine" Problem and How This Resolves It

A grade system that distributes 80% of products into D and E is not neutral. It is making an implicit claim: *most food in the market is structurally bad.* Whether or not this is directionally true for ultra-processed product categories, it misrepresents Bari's intended function.

Bari is designed to **distinguish** within the food landscape — to identify which products are structurally superior to which others, and to explain why. If 80% of products land at D/E, the system loses its ability to distinguish within that majority. Everything is "bad," and Bari cannot help users identify which bad product is less bad, or which moderately good product is actually quite good.

The v2 grade philosophy resolves this by:

1. **Introducing S-tier** to protect A-tier from ceiling compression. A-tier is no longer the aspirational maximum; it is a realistic description of excellent food.

2. **Lowering grade boundaries proportionally** so the full 0–100 range is utilized. Products no longer cluster at 40–55 while the 65–100 range is empty.

3. **Preserving strict E criteria.** E is not softened. The threshold moves from 40 to 35 — only five points. Products that genuinely earn E (heavy cap stacking, multiple red labels, severe engineering) remain E. This recalibration does not rescue bad products from E; it prevents mediocre products from being condemned to D when they belong in C.

4. **Anchoring A to structural excellence, not nutritional perfection.** Whole milk is structurally excellent. It is not perfect for every person. The grade should reflect the former, not the latter.

## Grade Reference Table

| Grade | Score | One-line meaning |
|-------|-------|-----------------|
| S | 90–100 | Structurally elite. Single-ingredient coherence, minimal processing, exceptional matrix integrity. Rare. |
| A | 80–89 | Structurally strong. Minimally engineered, coherent matrix, meaningful nutritional contribution. |
| B | 65–79 | Generally sound. Real tradeoffs present but not dominant. Structurally credible. |
| C | 50–64 | Mixed or moderate. One or two layers of concern; structural integrity limited but present. |
| D | 35–49 | Structurally weak or compromised. Significant concern in at least one layer. |
| E | below 35 | Dominant concern across multiple layers. Heavy engineering, severe cap stacking, regulatory failure. |

---

## Grade vs. Recommendation

Bari does not recommend foods. It grades them structurally. A person who needs a high-protein liquid meal may reasonably select a D-grade product that delivers 25g protein in a reconstructed matrix. Bari says: "This is D — heavily reconstructed, engineering signals present, but genuine protein delivery." The user's decision is their own.

The grade is information. It is not instruction.
