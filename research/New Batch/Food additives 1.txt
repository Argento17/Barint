Executive Summary
Food additives serve diverse technological functions but differ in safety profiles, evidence quality, and
regulatory status. We reviewed nine additive classes (emulsifiers, preservatives, sweeteners, polyols, colors,
flavor enhancers, stabilizers, gums, acidity regulators) and key examples within each. All are approved by
agencies like EFSA, FDA and JECFA at specified Acceptable Daily Intakes (ADIs) or “ADI not
specified” (generally safe) levels, but some remain controversial due to emerging research or vulnerable
subpopulations. For each class we summarize typical uses, toxicological data (absorption/metabolism,
sensitive groups), evidence strength (animal vs human studies), human outcomes (RCTs/cohorts), dose-
response (ADI, NOAEL, threshold), and regulation. We then assign tiers (Tier 1: strong concern; Tier 2:
moderate; Tier 3: neutral; Tier 4: context-dependent/beneficial) to representative additives with evidence-
based rationale. Finally, we present Bari logic for automated screening, including algorithmic rules, decision
thresholds, required inputs, and example scenarios. A comparative table distills key attributes (function,
ADI/NOAEL, tier , main outcomes, regulatory notes) for prominent additives. Our recommendations
prioritize primary regulatory sources (EFSA, JECFA, FDA, Codex) and recent meta-analyses; gaps and
controversies are highlighted for further research.
Emulsifiers
Function: Emulsifiers (e.g. mono-/diglycerides E471, lecithin E322, polysorbates E432/E436,
carboxymethylcellulose E466, carrageenan E407) enable oil–water mixtures (margarine, sauces, ice cream,
baked goods) to stay homogeneous【3†L216-L224】【3†L239-L247】. They also improve texture, volume
and shelf-life in breads, doughs and confections【3†L216-L224】【3†L237-L245】. Common natural
emulsifiers include lecithin (soy/egg) and gums; synthetic ones include polysorbates and polyricinoleates.
Safety (toxicology/ADME): Most emulsifiers are large molecules poorly absorbed. Lecithin hydrolyzes to
choline and fatty acids; soy-derived lecithin may carry allergenic protein residues. Polysorbate-80 (P80)
yields sorbitol and laurate; carboxymethylcellulose (CMC) is not digested, acting as fiber . ADME: e.g. ~99% of
ingested benzoates (relevant for polysorbates’ butyrate link) are metabolized to hippuric acid and excreted
【33†L218-L224】; cellulose ethers like CMC pass through gut unabsorbed【23†L75-L82】. No major
systemic toxicity has emerged: EFSA and JECFA assign “ADI not specified” or very high ADIs for most (e.g.
EFSA group-ADI “not specified” for cellulose emulsifiers【25†】). Vulnerable populations: inflammatory
bowel disease (IBD) patients or infants (especially any added to formulas) are sometimes mentioned as
more sensitive, but current data are limited.
Evidence strength: Mostly animal/mechanistic studies. Mouse studies (Chassaing et al.) show CMC/P80
induce low-grade gut inflammation, altered microbiota, and metabolic syndrome traits【11†L380-L388】
【11†L429-L437】. In vitro/ex vivo, CMC and P80 altered human gut microbiota composition and gene
expression toward a pro-inflammatory profile【19†L63-L71】. Human evidence is sparse. A recent
placebo-controlled RCT (60 adults) found that supplementing a low-emulsifier diet with single emulsifiers
(CMC, P80, carrageenan, lecithin) lowered fecal short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) but did not raise markers of gut
inflammation, lipopolysaccharide or metabolic risk【19†L63-L71】. Only carrageenan modestly increased
1
intestinal permeability. Overall, no direct human pathology from typical emulsifier intake has been
demonstrated; evidence is rated C (limited) due to few trials.
Human outcomes: In the 2026 RCT, mean SCFAs (butyrate, propionate) fell in emulsifier arms, potentially
reflecting microbiome shifts【19†L63-L71】. No significant change in fecal calprotectin, CRP or serum
markers vs placebo. A low-emulsifier diet lowered cholesterol (EFD period)【19†L63-L71】. Observationally,
high-processed diets (with many emulsifiers) correlate weakly with IBD or metabolic risk, but confounding
factors abound. No adverse effects like allergy or carcinogenesis have been reported at dietary exposure. 
Dose considerations: Typical intake is grams/day. EFSA “ADI not specified” implies no foreseeable risk at
realistic doses. For P80, regulatory evaluations noted likely limits because 1% in water (as used in mouse
studies) is much higher than human food levels. NOspecific human NOAEL; in mice, 1% in water (∼7 g/kg)
caused inflammation. In human RCT, emulsifier dose via brownies was not explicitly quantified, but likely
well below EFSA-approved levels. If we treat ADIs: e.g. none for lecithin (phospholipid), “not specified” for
many. 
Regulation: EFSA and FDA permit all common emulsifiers under specified uses. EFSA’s Panel has re-
evaluated many (e.g. E322 lecithin, E440 pectins) and kept ADIs at “not specified” or set high mg/kg limits
【3†L293-L301】. No bans exist, but any re-evaluation is ongoing (EU systematic re-eval by 2020). Labelling:
must list by E-number/name. 
Controversies & gaps: Debates center on the gut microbiome. Critics point to animal-model findings and
call for caution (e.g. food bloggers note “clean eating” to avoid emulsifiers). EFSA and FDA say available data
show no harm in humans; more research is needed on chronic low-dose effects, especially in IBD-prone
individuals. The main gap is lack of long-term human trials. Also, some acid esters (DATEM, SSL) could
release tartaric acid; high free tartarates have laxative potential. 
Summary: Emulsifiers improve texture and shelf-life, and are generally considered safe (Tier 3: neutral)
under current use. Lecithin (E322) is Tier 4 (benign, adds choline/fatty acids), synthetic surfactants like P80
and CMC are Tier 2 (moderate concern pending more evidence), and carrageenan is borderline Tier 2 due to
its disputed GI effects. We assign Tier 3 to most common emulsifiers, Tier 2 to those with suggestive
inflammatory signals, Tier 4 to nutritionally beneficial ones (e.g. lecithin), and Tier 1 to none in this class (no
acute toxins).
Preservatives
Function: Preservatives (nitrites/nitrates E249–252, benzoates E210–213, sulfites E220–228, sorbates E200/
E202, propionates, etc.) inhibit microbial growth and oxidative spoilage. Nitrates/nitrites preserve meats
and give color; benzoates prevent yeast/mold in acidic foods (soft drinks, pickles); sulfites prevent browning
and microbial growth (dried fruits, wines, beers). EDTA (E385) sequesters metals to slow oxidation (e.g. in
canned seafood).
Safety profile:
Nitrites/nitrates: Nitrate (NO₃⁻) is largely excreted but partly converted to nitrite (NO₂⁻) via oral
bacteria. NO₂⁻ can oxidize hemoglobin to methemoglobin and form nitrosamines (potential

carcinogens) in the stomach【29†L25-L34】. EFSA’s ADI: nitrate 3.7 mg/kg, nitrite 0.07 mg/kg (as
nitrite ion)【29†L42-L49】. JECFA same. Typical dietary nitrate is mostly from vegetables; cured
meats contribute added nitrate/nitrite. At high exposures, risk is methemoglobinemia in infants
(“blue baby”), a known hazard. In adults, conversion to carcinogenic nitrosamines is the main
theoretical risk. EFSA concluded that nitrosamine formation at permitted levels is of low concern
【30†L13-L22】; however , it noted uncertainties about long-term effects【30†L13-L22】. 
Benzoates: (Benzoic acid/salts) are rapidly absorbed and conjugated with glycine to hippuric acid;
>99% excreted【33†L218-L224】. No mutagenicity or teratogenicity at normal doses. EFSA set a
group ADI 5 mg/kg (benzoic acid equivalent)【33†L146-L154】 (JECFA 0–5 mg/kg). Sensitive
individuals may get skin or asthma reactions from benzoates. There is a controversial link to
hyperactivity: a UK study showed a small effect of certain dye/benzoate mixtures on child behavior
【44†L272-L281】, but EFSA deemed the evidence inconclusive and retained ADIs. 
Sulfites (sulfur dioxide E220 etc.): Sulfur dioxide and sulfite salts release SO₂, which can cause
bronchospasm in asthmatics and destroy thiamine (vitamin B1) in foods. EFSA (2022) found that
estimated intakes for high consumers (especially children and adults) might exceed safe levels,
using margin-of-exposure (MOE) analysis【35†L310-L318】. No ADI was established; instead, EFSA
set MOE benchmarks. EFSA noted animal studies showing nervous system effects at high doses
【35†L319-L324】. Allergic/intolerance reactions (e.g. in asthmatics) are recognized. 
Sorbic acid (E200) & salts: Metabolized to CO₂ and water; no absorption issues. Former ADI 0–25 mg/
kg (FDA GRAS), updated EFSA ADI 11 mg/kg (as sorbic acid) in 2019【37†L72-L77】. No major health
concerns at normal use. 
Others: Propionates (anti-mold in baked goods) have ADI ~10 mg/kg; EDTA is “ADI not specified” by
FDA/GRA.
Strength of evidence: Preservatives have been well-tested in rodents (subchronic/chronic) establishing
ADIs. Human evidence mainly comes from observational or case reports. Key RCT/cohort data:
Nitrites/nitrates: Cohort studies link high processed meat intake to cancer risk (colorectal), but
confounded by fat, cooking, heme iron. The consensus is that nitrate from veggies is beneficial
(blood pressure lowering via NO), whereas processed-meat nitrosamines remain debated. EFSA
found current ADIs protective【30†L25-L30】. 
Benzoates: Lacking RCTs. Ex vivo work: very high benzoate (10×ADI) had minimal effects on human
gut microbiota composition【33†L253-L262】. Hyperactivity trials (with dyes) showed only slight,
inconsistent effects【44†L272-L281】. 
Sulfites: Case reports of asthma attacks or anaphylaxis in sensitive individuals. No RCTs. 
Propionates/Sorbates: Generally no human concerns reported. 
Human outcomes: Aside from niche sensitivities, preservatives at regulated use cause no reported adverse
health effects in general populations. High sulfite intake is acknowledged to possibly impair the nervous
system【35†L319-L324】, but daily diets rarely approach those levels. 
Dose considerations: ADIs govern risk thresholds. For nitrates/nitrites, exceeding ADI (e.g. >20 mg/kg
nitrate) over time could allow harmful nitrosamine formation【29†L25-L34】. Benzoates’ ADI of 5 mg/kg is
not hard to exceed: a 70 kg adult could reach it with ~3.5 g/d, which could occur with multiple soft drinks
and pickled snacks. A 2025 study showed that even 35 g/L (10×ADI) benzoate markedly shifts some gut
bacteria but not overall diversity【33†L253-L262】.

Regulation: Nitrites and benzoates have specified maximum levels in foods (e.g. ≤150 mg/kg for nitrites in
meats). Sulfites must be declared if >10 mg/kg and are banned in fresh meat (because of high background
nitrate) in some regions. EU labelling requires “contains sulfites” for >10 ppm. US labels sulfite-preserved
foods under Food Allergen law for asthmatics. Some countries restrict colors and preservatives in baby
foods. 
Controversies/Gaps: The nitrite–nitrosamine–cancer link is controversial; recent IARC re-evaluations (2021)
classify processed meat (with nitrite) as carcinogenic, but largely attribute that to cooking-derived
compounds. The beneficial NO from nitrate complicates risk–benefit (at least 800 mg nitrate from
vegetables can lower BP). EFSA noted “benefits outweigh risks” of dietary nitrate for cardiovascular health
【30†L30-L34】. Sulfites lack data on chronic CNS effects; EFSA called for more study of sulfite neurotoxicity.
Benzoate as an antimicrobial may alter gut flora at high dose, but evidence to date suggests minimal
impact at realistic intakes【33†L253-L262】. 
Summary:
Nitrate/Nitrite (Tier 2): Moderate concern due to nitrosamine potential (Tier 2) – EFSA ADIs
protective【29†L42-L49】【30†L25-L32】. 
Benzoate (Tier 2): ADI 5 mg/kg, slight sensitivity/allergy risk, controversial hyperactivity link (Tier 2)
【44†L272-L281】【33†L146-L154】. 
Sulfites (Tier 1): Asthma/thiamine loss and EFSA’s MOE signal for high consumers (Tier 1)【35†L310-
L318】【35†L319-L324】. 
Sorbate (Tier 3): ADI 11 mg/kg, little evidence of harm – neutral (Tier 3). 
Propionate, EDTA (Tier 3): No red flags, Tier 3.
Artificial Sweeteners
Function: Artificial (non-nutritive) sweeteners (e.g. aspartame E951, saccharin E954, sucralose E955,
acesulfame K E950, neotame E961, advantame) provide high sweetness intensity with minimal calories.
Steviol glycosides (E960, from Stevia rebaudiana) are plant-based sweeteners. These enable “diet” foods/
beverages for calorie reduction or blood sugar control.
Safety profile:
Aspartame: Metabolized to phenylalanine, aspartic acid, and methanol. Persons with phenylketonuria
(PKU) cannot metabolize phenylalanine; thus products must warn. JECFA/EFSA ADI: 0–40 mg/kg
【39†L238-L241】 (e.g. a 70 kg adult could consume ~9–14 diet sodas/day without exceeding ADI
【39†L271-L277】). IARC (2023) classified aspartame as Group 2B (possibly carcinogenic) based on
“limited” evidence for liver cancer【39†L238-L241】【39†L265-L274】. JECFA reaffirmed ADI; WHO
noted “safety not a major concern at common doses” but called for more studies【39†L238-L241】
【39†L271-L278】. 
Sucralose: Mostly excreted unchanged; tiny percent metabolized to chlorinated compounds. ADI 0–
15 mg/kg (JECFA). Early concerns about microbiome/gut and glucose tolerance have been raised in
rodents, but human RCTs show sucralose does not significantly alter glycemic response in healthy
adults.

Saccharin: Excreted unchanged. ADI 0–5 mg/kg. Past rodent bladder-tumor findings at high doses
are not relevant to humans (via species-specific mechanisms); deemed safe by IARC and others. 
Acesulfame K: ADI 0–15 mg/kg. No specific human issues reported. 
Stevia (Steviol glycosides): ADI 0–4 mg/kg (expressed as steviol); largely non-toxic, some bitter
aftertaste at high dose.
Others (neotame, advantame): Very high sweetness power , very low ADIs, used in limited foods.
No toxicities such as organ damage are known at approved levels. Some claims of migraines or metabolic
effects remain unsubstantiated. 
Evidence strength: Many RCTs and meta-analyses exist:
A 2025 meta-analysis of RCTs (n≈1457) found no significant differences in weight, glucose, or lipid
outcomes between adults consuming artificially sweetened beverages (ASBs) versus unsweetened
controls【42†L349-L358】. This suggests metabolic neutrality in the short term. 
Other meta-analyses show modest weight reduction when replacing sugar with nonnutritive
sweeteners, but effects are small. Observational studies sometimes associate ASBs with obesity or
diabetes, but are likely confounded by reverse causality (overweight people switch to diet drinks)
【42†L349-L358】. 
Aspartame’s link to cancer: IARC’s classification is based on limited human data (two cohort signals
for hepatocellular carcinoma)【39†L265-L274】. No RCT addresses long-term cancer risk. JECFA sees
no need to lower ADI【39†L271-L277】.
Human outcomes:Glucose/insulin: In RCTs, artificial sweeteners yield lower postprandial glycemia than
sugar and have negligible insulin secretion, potentially beneficial for diabetics. Gut microbiota: Some rodent
studies show dysbiosis with certain sweeteners, but human data are mixed. A small study found sucralose
altered gut microbiome at very high doses; others found no effect. Body weight: Replacing sugar with
sweeteners may aid weight maintenance in the short term; long-term efficacy is unclear . Neurological/
other: Aspartame (which yields methanol) has been tested in many RCTs without finding evidence for
headache or cognition issues. A few individuals report headaches or allergic-like reactions, but trials often
show placebo-level effects. Safety signal: Phenylketonurics (0.5% of population) must avoid aspartame.
There are no proven adverse effects on pregnancy, children, or other vulnerable groups from acceptable
intakes, aside from the PKU issue.
Dose: Typical exposures are well below ADIs. For example, two cans of diet soda (~180 mg aspartame) are
<5% of a 40 mg/kg ADI for a 70 kg person【39†L271-L277】. Gestational exposure is not a concern at
dietary levels except PKU risk. 
Regulation: All major agencies have approved these sweeteners. Aspartame and saccharin are one of the
oldest approvals; newer ones (acesulfame, sucralose) went through modern risk assessments. Sweeteners
are generally permitted in foods and beverages with concentration limits. Stevia is also authorized (E960) in
EU and US. Labels must indicate presence of artificial sweeteners, and in EU products with steviol glycosides
must state “with sweetener”. 
Controversies/Gaps: The biggest controversy is cancer risk of aspartame. The IARC 2023 listing (Group 2B)
has raised public concern, though JECFA and regulators maintain the approved ADI is safe【39†L238-L241】
【39†L271-L277】. More epidemiological studies and mechanistic research on aspartame’s metabolite

Sugar Alcohols (Polyols)
Function: Polyols (e.g. sorbitol, xylitol, mannitol, erythritol, maltitol) are carbohydrate alcohols used as bulk
sweeteners and humectants. They provide sweetness with lower calories (except maltitol) and do not spike
blood sugar . Sorbitol and mannitol occur naturally in fruits; others are industrially produced. Common in
“sugar-free” candies, gum, diabetic foods.
Safety: Polyols are partially absorbed and metabolized (e.g. sorbitol → fructose in liver); the unabsorbed
portion is fermented in the colon. Excess intake leads to osmotic diarrhea, flatulence, and bloating. EU
mandates “excess may cause laxative effect” labels. No systemic toxicity. ADIs are generally “not
specified” (safe) because toxic effects only appear at high doses. Vulnerable groups: Irritable bowel
syndrome (IBS) patients may be sensitive (FODMAP content). Xylitol is extremely toxic to dogs, but harmless
to humans. 
Evidence: RCTs on polyols focus on GI tolerance: threshold is typically 20–40 g/dose (e.g. one might get
diarrhea if >30 g sorbitol). They can cause GI upset in those unaccustomed or with IBS. However , they don’t
raise blood glucose; some meta-analyses find polyol diets improve dental health (non-cariogenic). No
studies show serious long-term harm at moderate use. 
Dose: A single package of sugar-free gum (10 pieces) may contain ~1–2 g xylitol; so one would need dozens
for GI effects. Maltitol candies often warn about laxative risk if >30 g/day. There is no formal ADI, but JECFA
established that daily consumption up to ~10 g is without effect; 20 g causes mild effects, 40 g significant GI
upset. 
Regulation: All are approved as food additives (e.g. sorbitol E420). Labeling rules require “excessive
consumption may cause laxative effects”. No restrictions beyond these warnings.
Controversies: Polyols themselves are benign. The main issue is consumer awareness of the laxative
threshold and the potential of hidden polyols in “healthy” foods. Also, polyols can disrupt low-carb
(ketogenic) diets if not tracked.

Summary: Polyols are Tier 3 (neutral): safe and useful, though Tier 2 (caution) for high intakes or IBS-
prone individuals. Some (xylitol) have dental benefits (Tier 4 effect). No additive in this class merits Tier 1
concern.
Food Colors
Function: Additives like Tartrazine (E102, Yellow 5), Sunset Yellow (E110, Yellow 6), Allura Red (E129, Red 40),
Brilliant Blue (E133), and natural pigments (beta-carotene E160a, annatto E160b, paprika E160c, chlorophyll
E140) provide or restore color . They enhance appeal and compensate for loss of color due to processing.
Safety: Synthetic azo dyes have been scrutinized for hypersensitivity (especially urticaria/asthma in
salicylate-sensitive people) and neurobehavioral effects. ADIs (EFSA): Tartrazine 7.5, Sunset 5, Allura 7 mg/
kg. EFSA found no carcinogenic or genotoxic risk at permitted uses. Ponceau 4R (not in US) had equivocal
bladder tumor data in rodents but ADI set at 0.7 mg/kg. Natural colors (carotenes, anthocyanins, paprika)
have ADIs “not specified” or high (beta-carotene 5 mg/kg). 
Evidence: The Southampton study (McCann 2007) found that mixtures of six artificial colors plus benzoate
modestly increased hyperactivity in a general-child cohort【44†L272-L281】. EFSA re-evaluations (2008)
acknowledged “limited evidence of a small effect in some children”【44†L272-L281】 but overall ADIs were
upheld. No clear link to ADHD or cognitive deficits in population studies. Other RCTs (e.g. in preschoolers)
showed no consistent effect of single dyes. Except for hypersensitivity, no human morbidity has been linked
to dyes.
Dose: People rarely exceed ADI from diet alone, unless very heavy consumers of colored candies/
beverages. For example, Tartrazine ADI (7.5 mg/kg) is ~525 mg/d for 70 kg adult. A typical soda contains
~20 mg per can, so 25 cans would be needed to reach ADI. 
Regulation: EU requires health warning label (in black print) on products containing E102, E104, E110, E122,
E124, E129: “may have an adverse effect on activity and attention in children.” US has no such warning.
Some countries ban specific dyes (e.g. Norway banned Yellow 5, Yellow 6; certain red dyes banned in
Singapore). Codex Alimentarius permits them with limits (often following EFSA). 
Controversies: The hyperactivity link remains debated. Critics push for removing artificial dyes from
children’s diets as a precaution. EFSA and FDA say current uses are safe for the general population
【44†L272-L281】. An unresolved gap is the effect of exposure in sensitive subpopulations, and combined
effects of multiple dyes and additives.
Summary: Synthetic colors are Tier 2 (moderate concern) due to possible hyperactivity in sensitive
children. Benzoate and color mixtures got a small warning in EFSA’s evaluation【44†L272-L281】. Allura Red
and Tartrazine are Tier 2. Natural colors (e.g. carotenes, anthocyanins) are Tier 4 (beneficial antioxidant
context). Overall, color additives are regulated to ensure ADIs are not exceeded; hyperactive-susceptible
children are a noted at-risk group (Tier 2 concern).

Flavor Enhancers
Function: Substances like Monosodium Glutamate (MSG, E621) and disodium 5′-guanylate (E626) and 5′-
inosinate (E631) enhance umami/savory taste. Yeast extracts (rich in glutamate) serve similarly. They allow
reduced use of salt or fat while maintaining flavor .
Safety: MSG is extensively studied. Early anecdotal “Chinese Restaurant Syndrome” (headache, flushing)
was not confirmed in controlled trials (only extremely high IV doses caused transient symptoms). JECFA ADI:
“not specified” (safe) at normal dietary use. MSG is metabolized as natural glutamate; it is a
neurotransmitter precursor but does not cross the blood–brain barrier in normal amounts. Disodium 5′-
nucleotides similarly have no known toxicity. 
Evidence: No high-quality RCT has linked MSG in foods to chronic disease. Meta-analyses of headaches find
no effect at normal exposures. Animal studies using massive doses showed no organ toxicity. Some
nutritionists argue MSG may trigger appetite; others find that it can reduce salt use and sodium intake
(potential benefit). 
Human outcomes: MSG in food causes no clear symptoms in most people. Rare idiosyncratic reactions
(flushing, nausea, sweating) can occur , but double-blind studies showed these only at high intakes (~3–5 g,
much above culinary levels) and even then inconsistently. It does slightly raise plasma glutamate, but
homeostasis clears it quickly. There is no evidence linking MSG to obesity or diabetes in humans; some
cohort studies show neutral or even protective associations (due to lowered salt). 
Dose: Typical consumption is 0.5–1 g/day in cuisines using it (much lower than eg. dietary protein-bound
glutamate). No toxicity threshold found in animal studies up to 10% diet (unrealistic). One concern is the 
non-enzymatic reaction of MSG in prepared foods might produce trace amounts of carcinogenic
compounds (e.g. trace 3-deoxyglucosone), but evidence is speculative.
Regulation: MSG is generally recognized as safe (GRAS by FDA) and authorized worldwide. It must be
labeled as “monosodium glutamate” (US) or “flavour enhancer E621” (EU). In Sweden, MSG once had
warning labeling; now removed. Disodium nucleotides and yeast extracts are uncontroversial authorized
additives.
Controversies: A few individuals claim MSG sensitivity; this remains unproven at normal dietary levels.
Some believe free glutamate triggers gut growth or endocrine effects, but studies do not support this at
food doses. The World Health Organization did not list MSG in any “hazard” category. Research gaps: long-
term effects on metabolism or neurodegeneration have not been meaningfully studied in humans.
Summary: MSG and related enhancers are Tier 3 (neutral). No regulatory body views them as toxic. We
classify MSG as Tier 3 (neutral) given lack of credible harm, with a note that very high intake anecdotes
place it near Tier 2 if someone self-identifies as sensitive (but this is rare). 
Stabilizers and Gums
Function: These are thickening or gelling agents that stabilize textures. Stabilizers (often overlap with
emulsifiers) include pectin (E440, fruit jams), alginate (E401 sodium alginate, dairy gels), agar (E406,
8
vegetarian gelling), and carrageenan (E407, dairy analogs). Gums (E412–E425) like guar (E412), xanthan
(E415), gum arabic (E414), locust bean gum (E410) add viscosity and bind water . They prevent syneresis in
yogurts, thicken soups, or suspend particulates in sauces. 
Safety: Most are indigestible polysaccharides similar to dietary fiber . They undergo fermentation in the
colon. At high intakes they may cause GI symptoms (e.g. guar gum can cause diarrhea). Pectin and alginate
are widely considered fiber supplements (e.g. pectin 20 g/day reduces cholesterol). No toxic metabolites.
Regulatory bodies often do not specify ADIs (“ADI not specified” for many).
Carrageenan is controversial: high-molecular-weight carrageenan (food-grade, λ- or κ-carrageenan) is
considered safe, but degraded carrageenan (poligeenan, not used in food) caused tumors in rodents. Some
animal studies give high doses of food-grade carrageenan and reported gut inflammation and possible
promotion of tumors in the presence of other carcinogens. EFSA reviewed carrageenan several times and
kept it authorized, though a formal re-evaluation for infants was underway.
Evidence: Human studies: pectin lowers lipids (Cochrane review); no adverse reports. Alginate used for
reflux, safe. Xanthan and guar gum slightly lower postprandial glucose and have prebiotic fiber effects in
RCTs. Carrageenan: a few small trials of carrageenan-containing yogurts showed no GI harm in healthy
people (even some anti-HP infection trials). Meta-analyses: none have linked stabilizers/gums to chronic
disease. A 2025 ex vivo trial (see emulsifiers) showed carrageenan raised permeability.
Dose: Food uses are low (0.1–1% of product). For example, 5 g daily of guar is common in weight-loss
regimens; at 30 g can cause diarrhea. No formal NOAEL established (they generally have wide safety
margins). 
Regulation: All common gums/stabilizers are approved (e.g. guar E412, xanthan E415, pectin E440).
Maximum use levels are specified (e.g. up to ~15 g/kg in some confectionery). EU mandates labeling
(“stabilizer: E440”). In the US they may appear under generic names (e.g. “pectin”, “guar gum”). There is no
ADI; safety is assumed at GRAS levels.
Controversies: Carrageenan’s safety is debated in natural-health communities (some advise avoiding it,
calling it an “inflammatory” agent). Scientific bodies (EFSA, FDA, JECFA) find no current evidence to restrict it.
The need to re-evaluate carrageenan with modern methods is recognized. Some also question gum arabic’s
occupational allergy in manufacturing (not food risk). 
Summary: Most stabilizers/gums are Tier 4 (beneficial/context-dependent) due to their fiber-like
properties. Pectin, xanthan, guar , alginate improve texture and may offer health benefits (fiber).
Carrageenan is Tier 2 (moderate) pending human data on chronic inflammation; other gums are Tier 3/4.
No Tier 1 agents here.
Acidity Regulators
Function: Acidity regulators (acids and their salts, e.g. citric acid E330, malic E296, lactic E270, fumaric E297,
phosphoric acid E338) control pH and add tartness. They preserve foods by lowering pH (inhibiting
microbes) and adjust flavor (e.g. citric acid in beverages, phosphoric acid in colas, lactic acid in fermented
foods). Carbonates and bicarbonates (E500) are also acidity regulators (raising pH).
9
Safety: Most are naturally occurring organic acids with low toxicity. Citric, malic, lactic, acetic acids have no
ADIs (ADI “not specified”) and are normal metabolites. Phosphoric acid has been scrutinized: excessive
dietary phosphate (from cola drinks or processed foods) may affect bone mineral density and kidney health.
EFSA set a safe upper intake for total phosphorus (OOP) in 2019, but specifically for additives, uses are
generally within expected diets. Excess ingestion of any strong acid can erode dental enamel (e.g. frequent
sour candy ingestion), but regulated use is considered safe.
Evidence: There is minimal evidence of harm. Some studies link high cola (phosphoric acid) intake to lower
bone density in older women, but these diets also replace milk. Rare case of lactic acidosis from massive
ingestion of baking soda (sodium bicarbonate) exists, but irrelevant to normal use. A small proportion of
people may experience GI upset (heartburn) if consuming large acidic drinks.
Dose: These acids are generally consumed in grams: e.g. a lemonade might have 1–2% citric acid (~1–2 g
per 250 ml). ADI “not specified” suggests no risk at any realistic consumption. Phosphoric acid in soda is
~50 mg/100 ml, so 500 ml cola provides ~250 mg phosphorus, well below the daily RDI (~700 mg P in diet).
Regulation: All are universally permitted, often without numerical limits (using “quantum satis” or broad
categories). Labeling may indicate “acidity regulator (citric acid)”. No restrictions beyond normal labeling;
these are seen as GRAS/ESAs. 
Controversies: The main discussion is around soft drinks: phosphoric acid consumption vs bone health and
CKD (chronic kidney disease). Evidence is mixed, and confounders exist (caffeine, lifestyle). EFSA and FDA do
not view these acid levels as risky. Non-acidic alternatives (citric acid) have replaced some phosphoric use
due to health perceptions. People with GERD might avoid citrus acids. 
Summary: Acidity regulators are Tier 3/4: essentially safe and even beneficial for flavor and preservation.
Citric, malic, lactic, acetic, bicarbonates are Tier 4 (natural/benign). Phosphoric acid is Tier 3 (neutral) unless
consumed extremely (like daily liters of cola). There are no Tier 1 concerns in this class.

Based on the above evidence, we propose:
Tier 1 (Strong Concern):Sulfites (E220–228) – due to EFSA’s finding that high consumers (esp.

Bari Logic and Decision Framework
To implement additive risk assessment in Bari, we propose the following algorithmic structure:
Inputs:
Additive identity (e.g. “E250 sodium nitrite” or common name) 
Dose per serving (mg) and consumption frequency 
User parameters (body weight, special populations like children, pregnancy) 
Retrieve Data: Look up the additive’s class, ADI (mg/kg), tier assignment, key effects, and regulatory
status from our database. 
Decision Rules (pseudocode):
FOR each additive in product:
    Tier = lookupTier(additive)
    ADI = lookupADI(additive)
    dailyExposure = dosePerServing * servingsPerDay / bodyWeight
    IF Tier == 1 THEN
        mark HighConcern
    ELSE IF Tier == 2 THEN
        IF ADI exists AND dailyExposure > 0.5*ADI THEN
            mark ModerateConcern
        ELSE IF user has vulnerability (e.g. asthma for sulfites) THEN
            mark ModerateConcern
        ELSE
            mark LowConcern
    ELSE IF Tier == 3 THEN
        mark LowConcern
    ELSE IF Tier == 4 THEN
        mark LowConcern (potentially beneficial)
- Threshold example: For preservatives like nitrite, use 0.5×ADI as a conservative trigger . For sulfites, any
detectable amount for an asthmatic is a red flag.

Example Use Cases:
User consumes a soda with 10 mg sodium benzoate per serving, drinks 3 servings/day, weight 70 kg.
Lookup: Benzoate (Tier 2, ADI 5 mg/kg). Daily dose = 30 mg. 30/(705) = 8.6% of ADI. <50%. No
vulnerability flagged. Result: Tier 2 but exposure low → Low/Moderate concern*.
User with asthma has snack with sulfites (5 mg/serving) 2/day.
Lookup: Sulfite (Tier 1, no safe ADI). Result: Tier 1 → High concern, advise caution or elimination.
Pregnant woman eats yogurt with 200 mg carrageenan (E407).
Tier: Carrageenan Tier 2 (potential inflammation). 200 mg is low (ADI NS). Result: Moderate concern;
if GI symptoms, reduce; else ok.
Child weight 20 kg eats candy with Tartrazine 3 mg and benzoate 50 mg.
Tartrazine: Tier 2, ADI 7.5 mg/kg = 150 mg for child; consumed 3 mg (2% ADI). Benzoate: Tier 2, ADI
100 mg; consumed 50 mg (50% ADI). Exposure flagged for benzoate (exactly 50%). Mixed exposures
might be cumulative but handled additively in future versions. Result: Tartrazine low concern;
Benzoate at threshold → Moderate caution.
User subs for sugar: drinks 2 diet sodas (aspartame 180 mg total), weight 70 kg.
Aspartame ADI 2800 mg (70×40). Intake 180 mg < 10% ADI. Tier 2 (due to IARC), but exposure trivial. 
Result: Low concern with note on PKU (irrelevant to non-PKU).
Conclusions and Next Steps
Our analysis integrates regulatory benchmarks and current science into a tiered framework. Tier 1 additives
(sulfites) should trigger the strictest scrutiny; Tier 2 additives (e.g. nitrates, artificial colors, some
emulsifiers) warrant moderate caution, especially at high doses or in susceptible people. Most others are
Tier 3 or 4 (no action needed beyond normal labels).
