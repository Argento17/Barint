**Date:** June 1, 2026

## Finding 1: Emulsifier-Specific Gut Effects Confirmed in Placebo-Controlled RCT

### Summary
A placebo-controlled RCT (n=60) tested five common emulsifiers — carboxymethylcellulose (CMC), polysorbate-80, carrageenan, soy lecithin, and native rice starch — against placebo following a 2-week emulsifier-free washout. Key finding: **carrageenan specifically increased transcellular intestinal permeability** vs. baseline. **CMC produced the broadest reduction in SCFA concentrations** (acetate, propionate, butyrate all lower), with other emulsifiers showing similar but sub-significant trends. Systemic inflammation markers (CRP, LBP, fecal calprotectin) were not significantly elevated across the board, suggesting gut microbiome disruption precedes detectable systemic inflammation at typical dietary doses.

- **Published:** Clinical Gastroenterology and Hepatology, 2025
  - [Abstract](https://www.cghjournal.org/article/S1542-3565(25)00698-6/abstract) | [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40816342/)

### Evidence Strength
**High** — placebo-controlled RCT in healthy humans, emulsifier-free washout design controls confounders

### BSIP2 Relevance
Current additive penalty scoring likely treats emulsifiers as a single class. This RCT supports a **tiered emulsifier penalty** where carrageenan (E 407) and CMC (E 466) warrant higher deductions than soy lecithin or native rice starch.

### Potential Implementation
Differentiate the additive score for carrageenan (+1 tier penalty) and CMC (+0.5 tier penalty) vs. soy lecithin (near-neutral at these doses). Native rice starch may be reclassified from additive to functional ingredient.

### Estimated Impact on Scoring
**Medium-High** — carrageenan appears widely in dairy alternatives, deli meats, and infant formula; reclassification would affect many evaluated products.

## Finding 2: Protein Bar Food Matrix Degrades Protein Quality to 47–81% of Label Claims

### Summary
A large-scale study of **1,641 commercial protein bars** measured DIAAS using in vitro INFOGEST digestion. Protein digestibility in the bar matrix ranged from **47–81%**, substantially below the digestibility of the same protein sources evaluated in isolation. The matrix effect was attributed to interactions between protein and co-ingredients (carbohydrates, fats, fibers, sugars, heat processing during manufacturing). Collagen-containing bars performed worst. High-protein claim thresholds (≥20% calories from protein) were met by 81% of bars, but actual bioavailable protein was materially lower in nearly all cases.

- **Published:** Nature Scientific Reports, March 2025
  - [Scientific Reports](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-94072-4) | [Fortune coverage May 30 2025](https://fortune.com/well/2025/05/30/protein-bars-quality-study/)

### Evidence Strength
**High** — large sample (1,641 bars), validated in vitro DIAAS methodology (INFOGEST)

### BSIP2 Relevance
Direct challenge to any scoring that uses label protein content as proxy for protein quality. Processed/bar format suppresses DIAAS independent of the ingredient source declared on the label.

### Potential Implementation
Apply a **food matrix processing penalty to protein quality scoring** for bars and similarly highly processed protein formats. Label protein quantity should be discounted by an estimated 20–50% to reflect true bioavailability. Collagen inclusion should trigger an additional penalty given its incomplete amino acid profile and lowest measured matrix digestibility.

### Estimated Impact on Scoring
**High** — protein bars are a common evaluated product category; this changes protein contribution calculations materially.

## Finding 3: Fiber Diversity Produces 32.8% Synergistic SCFA Uplift vs. Single-Fiber Scoring

### Summary
An in vitro fecal fermentation study (n=33 participants, 4 health conditions: healthy, Crohn's, UC, Parkinson's) found that a **fiber mixture produced 32.8 ± 20.1% more total SCFAs** than the arithmetic expectation of individual fiber contributions. Synergy was observed across all four health conditions. Individuals with the strongest synergy were enriched in *Lachnospiraceae* and *Ruminococcaceae* — key butyrate producers. Many individuals showed >50% synergy; some exceeded 80%.

- **Published:** Nutrients, December 13, 2025
  - [Nutrients](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/17/24/3904) | [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12735490/)

### Evidence Strength
**Medium** — robust in vitro design with human fecal inocula across diverse health states; not an in vivo RCT

### BSIP2 Relevance
Scoring that evaluates each fiber type independently underestimates the functional value of products with diverse fiber profiles. A single high-dose fiber (e.g., inulin only) may score similarly to a product with 3–4 complementary fibers, despite the latter producing ~33% more SCFAs in practice.

### Potential Implementation
Introduce a **fiber diversity bonus modifier** — products containing 3+ distinct fermentable fiber types receive an upward score adjustment reflecting demonstrated synergistic fermentation potential. Products relying on a single isolated fiber do not qualify for the modifier.

### Estimated Impact on Scoring
**Medium** — affects whole grain / legume / fermented food products with diverse fiber profiles vs. supplements and bars using single isolated fibers.

## Finding 4: FDA Formally Reassessing BHA Safety — Regulatory Signal for Preserved Food Scoring

### Summary
The FDA launched a post-market safety reassessment of **butylated hydroxyanisole (BHA, E 320)** on February 10, 2026. The National Toxicology Program has designated BHA as "reasonably anticipated to be a human carcinogen" based on forestomach tumors in rodents. The FDA requested safety data with a comment deadline of April 13, 2026. BHA is used as an antioxidant preservative in oils, fats, cereals, snack foods, and food-contact packaging. The formal FDA reassessment marks an escalation in regulatory posture after decades of GRAS status.

- **Sources:**
  - [FDA Press Release](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/press-announcements/fda-launches-assessment-bha-common-food-chemical-preservative)
  - [Federal Register](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/02/11/2026-02761/butylated-hydroxyanisole-bha-request-for-information)
  - [Food Navigator](https://www.foodnavigator-usa.com/Article/2026/02/11/fda-launches-reassessment-of-bha-food-preservative-amid-safety-concerns/)

### Evidence Strength
**Medium** — formal regulatory action based on NTP animal evidence; human carcinogenicity not established; extrapolation to human dietary doses contested

### BSIP2 Relevance
Products containing BHA on the ingredient label should carry a **precautionary penalty** consistent with how BSIP2 treats ingredients under active regulatory scrutiny. BHA should be differentiated from BHT, which is not currently under FDA reassessment.

### Potential Implementation
Flag BHA-containing products in the additive penalty tier. Escalate penalty weight if FDA action results in restriction or GRAS revocation post-2026. Monitor the GRAS overhaul proposed rule expected spring 2026 for broader implications.

### Estimated Impact on Scoring
**Low-Medium** — BHA present in a subset of shelf-stable snack, oil, and cereal products; scoring impact proportional to prevalence in evaluated product set.

## Finding 5 (Monitoring Signal — Not Yet Actionable): Sucralose Shows Greater Microbiome Disruption Than Stevia in Mice

### Summary
A mouse study found that sucralose produced broader and more persistent microbiome disruption than stevia, with SCFA reductions and altered expression of *Tlr4*, *Tnf*, and *Srebp1* persisting into F1/F2 generations not directly exposed. **Expert consensus flags this as insufficient to revise human scoring**: doses were supra-physiological, mouse-to-human gut microbiome extrapolation is unreliable, the transgenerational mechanism is unclear (epigenetic vs. microbiome transfer), and existing human RCTs show minimal gut microbiome impact from both sweeteners at physiological doses.

- **Published:** Frontiers in Nutrition, January 30, 2026
  - [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2026.1694149/full)
  - [Expert reactions — Scimex](https://www.scimex.org/newsfeed/exspert-reaction-mouse-study-suggests-artificial-sweetners-may-impact-the-next-generation)
  - [ISA rebuttal](https://www.sweeteners.org/new-animal-study-on-sucralose-and-stevia-does-not-alter-scientific-consensus-on-their-safety/)

### Evidence Strength
**Low** for human scoring purposes — animal model, supra-physiological doses, no human RCT corroboration

### BSIP2 Relevance
Does not support changing sucralose or stevia penalty scores at this time. Consistent with the existing pattern of sucralose warranting more caution than stevia, but no calibration change is warranted.

### Potential Implementation
No change. Flag for re-evaluation if a human RCT on microbiome effects at physiological doses publishes with consistent findings.

### Estimated Impact on Scoring
**No change recommended**

## Regulatory Context Note: EFSA Mandatory FAIM Tool, Effective July 2026

EFSA published updated food additive authorization guidance in January 2026 (effective July 20, 2026), making the **FAIM (Food Additive Intake Model) tool mandatory** for exposure assessment in all new additive authorization applications. The update also introduces nanoparticle-specific assessment requirements. This is a methodology change affecting how additive safety dossiers are evaluated in the EU — not a direct change to the permitted additive list — but it signals that EFSA is tightening exposure assessment standards and that certain additives may face re-evaluation pressure.

- **Sources:** [EFSA Journal](https://efsa.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.2903/j.efsa.2026.9778) | [Bird & Bird analysis](https://www.twobirds.com/en/insights/2026/efsa-rewrites-the-playbook-what-the-2026-food-additive-guidance-means-for-your-authorisation-strateg)
